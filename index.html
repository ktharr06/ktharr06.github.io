Current/Past Projects

The Microbiome Portrait Experiment

Everywhere we go we are surrounded by microbes – bacteria, archaea, protists, algae, fungi, and even micro-animals. Microbes can be found on the surfaces of our dwellings, in the bodies of water we build our cities next to, in the soil in which we plant our food, and, it turns out, even in our own bodies. The population of bacterial cells that live in a particular environment is called a microbiome. In the Microbiome Portrait Experiment, students will generate a “portrait” of their own microbiome by sequencing specific regions of the 16S rRNA gene of bacteria captured by swabbing the body at different sites and comparing the DNA sequences in the sample to those of known bacteria.

Genetics of Race

The Genetics of Race project further explores an idea from the Human Genome Project and PBS: that there is no genetic basis for the racial categories that people use today. Students will do this by comparing their own mitochondrial DNA to each other and seeing whether there is a correlation with race. This project will not reveal any unwanted information regarding health, and the region of mitochondrial DNA that will be used is popular among anthropologists who study similar topics.

The Mutations Investigation Experiment

When cells divide, faithful transfer of genomic information from mother to daughter cells is essential. If mistakes occur in replication and are not corrected for, the nucleotide sequence is changed – a genetic mutation occurs. Therefore, cells have DNA repair systems to ensure that DNA is replicated consistently and that fidelity is maintained. In the Mutations Investigations Experiment (MIE), students will study mutations that occur randomly during replication by sequencing a plasmid that has been transformed into a strain of E. coli that does not have fully functional DNA repair systems.

The Microbiome Portrait Experiment

Everywhere we go we are surrounded by microbes -- bacteria, archaea, protists, algea, fungi, and even micro-animals. Microbes can be found on the surfaces of our dwellings, in the bodies of water we build our cities next to, in the soil in which we plant our food, and, it turns out, even in our own bodies. The Microbiome Portrait Experiment allows students to investigate the microbiomes present on their hard palates and behind their ear (retroauricular crease). Students can compare microbiome composition to determine how similar their microbiomes are to each other, and how similar they are to samples from other students.


The Mutations Investigation Experiment

In the Mutations Investigation Experiment, students will study mutations that occur randomly during replication. Students will be responsible for a variety of laboratory techniques including transformations, culturing bacteria, running polymerase chain reactions (PCR), and more!
